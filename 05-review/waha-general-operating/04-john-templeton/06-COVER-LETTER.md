Dear Heather, Matthew, and the Religion, Science, and Society programs team,

Sir John Templeton believed that humility in the face of the cosmos's complexity — scientific humility and spiritual humility together — was the beginning of wisdom. He funded inquiry, not conclusions. He trusted that rigorous investigation of life's biggest questions would produce wonder, not diminish it.

We are submitting this proposal in that spirit. We have observed something remarkable and don't fully understand why it works.

Since 2019, Waha has deployed a free, audio-based mobile app that enables any person — literate or not, trained or not, with internet access or not — to facilitate a Discovery Bible Study group by pressing play on their phone. The model is radically simple. What it has produced is not simple at all. From 16 groups to tens of thousands, across 177 countries and 46+ languages, in communities ranging from dying Swedish churches to underground cells in restricted-access nations to Hindu villages in rural India — the same tool produces the same phenomenon: people encountering Scripture together, and groups multiplying.

We don't have a rigorous explanation for why it works across such disparate contexts. We have data suggesting it does, and testimonials that illustrate it vividly. What we don't have is the systematic, peer-reviewed analysis that would tell the world — and tell us — what is actually happening, why it's replicable, and what conditions produce multiplication versus plateau.

This proposal asks the John Templeton Foundation to fund that inquiry. "Measuring the Impact of Audio-Based Scripture Engagement on Disciple-Making Movements in Oral Cultures" is a 24-month mixed-methods study, conducted in partnership with [Academic Partner / Dr. Name], that will analyze Waha's engagement data across 177 countries and conduct field ethnography in 5-10 deployment contexts. We will measure what we don't yet know: the rate and predictors of movement formation; the human flourishing outcomes among participants; the role of audio-first, facilitation-free access in contexts where literacy, internet, and pastoral training are absent.

Templeton has already funded the study of religious innovation, oral culture spirituality, and technology's effect on character formation — in pieces. This proposal integrates all three into a single study, with an unprecedented dataset and global field access. The findings will be published open-access and disseminated through Waha's partner network of 15+ leading missions organizations, including YouVersion, Wycliffe Bible Translators, and Biblica.

We believe Sir John Templeton would have found this question worth funding. We hope the Foundation agrees.
