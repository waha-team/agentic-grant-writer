## Online Funding Inquiry (OFI) Content

### Project Title
Measuring the Impact of Audio-Based Scripture Engagement on Disciple-Making Movements in Oral Cultures: A Mixed-Methods Study Across 10 Language Contexts

### Why the Project Is Important (OFI field — ~250 words)

How does removing literacy, clergy training, and internet access as requirements for Scripture engagement change the rate, depth, and sustainability of spiritual formation and community transformation?

This question has never been answered empirically. The global church has extensive experience with text-based, pastor-led Bible study. It has almost no systematic data on what happens when Scripture becomes accessible in audio format — facilitatable by anyone, in any language, offline, on a mobile phone — to communities where oral learning is primary.

The Waha platform represents an unprecedented natural experiment. Since 2019, Waha has deployed audio-based Discovery Bible Study curriculum to 82,000+ users across 177 countries in 46+ languages, with near-zero marketing spend. Every session generates data: language, location, lesson, engagement duration. Every community that forms around Waha generates a case study: groups that multiply, leaders who emerge, communities that transform in documented ways (addiction ended, marriages restored, persecuted faith communities sustained, entire villages converted through indigenous-led home groups).

No one has analyzed this data systematically. No academic study has asked: What are the predictors of disciple-making movement formation in Waha-using communities? What character and flourishing outcomes are measurable among participants? Does the audio-first, facilitation-free model produce different outcomes than text-based alternatives — and if so, why?

This study produces the first rigorous, peer-reviewed analysis of a significant religious innovation — and does so in precisely the populations (oral learners, restricted-access contexts, emerging-nation communities) where traditional religious research has the least data and the world has the most urgent need.

### 2. Statement of Need: The Research Gap

#### What We Know

Scripture engagement scholars have documented for decades that regular Bible reading and study correlates with positive outcomes: increased prosocial behavior, reduced depression and anxiety, stronger community cohesion, and greater life satisfaction (Barna Group, American Bible Society, multiple peer-reviewed studies). The research base is richest for literate, Western, churchgoing populations who engage Scripture through reading and pastor-led instruction.

#### What We Don't Know

The field is nearly silent on three critical questions:

**1. What happens in oral cultures?** An estimated 70% of the world's population are oral learners — people who learn primarily through hearing and conversation rather than reading. For these populations, text-based Bible study is not merely inconvenient; it is functionally inaccessible. The research on Scripture engagement's effects on flourishing is built almost entirely on literate populations. The largest populations of "unreached" communities are precisely the oral learners the existing research doesn't cover.

**2. What happens without clergy?** Every study of Scripture engagement efficacy is confounded by the presence of trained facilitators — pastors, missionaries, teachers. We don't know what happens when the trained leader is removed from the equation entirely and any person can facilitate a discussion group by pressing play on a mobile app. Does the outcome change? Does the multiplication rate change? Does the character formation process differ?

**3. What happens when technology delivers Scripture in under-resourced contexts?** Mobile penetration in Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and the Middle East has transformed communication — but religious research on technology-mediated Scripture engagement lags years behind this reality. How does an audio-based mobile app perform as a vector for disciple-making movement formation compared to text-based alternatives or in-person instruction?

#### Why Now

The Waha platform provides, for the first time, the infrastructure to answer all three questions simultaneously. Its 82,000+ users across 177 countries in 46+ languages generate quantitative data at a scale no researcher has previously had access to. Its documented movement formation in the Philippines, Middle East, India, Africa, and Europe provides qualitative case studies for ethnographic depth. And its ongoing deployment into 69 more languages in the pipeline creates a prospective study opportunity.

This is a rare convergence: a significant, measurable religious innovation at global scale, with data available, and researchers not yet engaged. This study closes that gap.

### 3. Research Questions

**Primary questions:**
1. What is the rate of disciple-making movement formation (defined as: groups that multiply to 2nd and 3rd generation without direct facilitator involvement) among Waha user communities across 5-10 language deployment contexts?
2. What individual, relational, and contextual factors predict movement formation vs. plateau in Waha-using communities?

**Secondary questions:**
3. What measurable human flourishing outcomes (character change indicators, community cohesion, prosocial behavior, conflict reduction) are associated with sustained participation in Waha Discovery Bible Study groups?
4. Does the facilitation-free, audio-first model produce different engagement patterns, retention rates, or flourishing outcomes compared to literacy-dependent alternatives in the same cultural contexts?
5. How does the security and offline functionality of Waha affect Scripture engagement in restricted-access or persecution contexts, and what does this tell us about technology's role in enabling religious practice in constrained environments?

### 4. Methodology

**Design:** 24-month mixed-methods study with quantitative and qualitative components.

**Phase 1 (Months 1-6): Quantitative baseline analysis**
- Analysis of Waha's existing engagement data across all 46+ deployed languages
- Identification of 5-10 language/region contexts for deep-study (selected for geographic, cultural, and data richness diversity)
- Development of survey instruments for measuring human flourishing indicators (character change, community cohesion, prosocial behavior) in each target context
- IRB approval for human subjects research

**Phase 2 (Months 7-18): Field ethnography and survey**
- Ethnographic field research in 5-10 selected deployment contexts
- In-depth interviews with Waha DBS facilitators, participants, and community leaders
- Survey administration to Waha user communities measuring flourishing outcomes at baseline and 6-month follow-up
- Longitudinal tracking of movement formation indicators (group multiplication rates, generational depth, leader emergence)

**Phase 3 (Months 19-24): Analysis and dissemination**
- Quantitative analysis of engagement data and survey results
- Qualitative thematic analysis of ethnographic field notes and interviews
- Integration of quantitative and qualitative findings
- Production of academic paper (peer-reviewed journal submission), conference presentations, and practitioner guide for faith communities
- Public presentation of findings to Waha's partner network (YouVersion, Wycliffe, Biblica, 24:14, IMB)

**Data sources:**
- Waha app engagement data (installs, sessions, lesson completions, user-reported group formations)
- Field interviews and ethnographic observation in 5-10 target communities
- Validated survey instruments adapted for cultural contexts (measuring flourishing indicators)
- Partner organization case documentation (Wycliffe, Biblica, 24:14, regional coordinators)

### 5. Evidence of the Phenomenon Under Study

The phenomenon this study will investigate — audio-based Scripture discovery catalyzing disciple-making movement formation — is already documented in Waha's field reports. Research will transform these anecdotal observations into systematic, generalizable findings.

**Philippines:** 12 trained believers grew 30 Discovery Bible Study groups to 90 groups in 50 days — with 20% of groups being third-generation (groups started by people who were not directly trained by the original 12). This multiplication rate is the key phenomenon of interest.

**Middle East / North Africa:** "We have seen over 100 churches start because of Waha. Many of these churches have multiplied to the 4th and 5th generation." — Maya, Muslim background believer. What conditions produced this 5th-generation multiplication? What can be replicated?

**Sweden:** A dying church (average age 60+, projecting closure within 15 years) launched 562 Discovery Bible Studies within 3 months of Waha's Swedish release — through the initiative of church members, not missionaries. What is the mechanism by which a tool produces this level of lay empowerment?

**India:** Entire villages moved from active persecution of Christians to mass baptism through Waha-facilitated home groups, combined with practical community service. What is the relationship between community service, audio Scripture discovery, and village-wide conversion? How does "gather, press play, discuss" function differently from conventional evangelism in resistant Hindu contexts?

**Restricted-access nations:** Documented cases of former militants encountering Waha groups and abandoning violent extremism after engaging with Scripture. What is happening psychologically and spiritually? Can this be measured and modeled?

These documented cases — across radically different cultural contexts — point to a consistent pattern: audio-based, facilitation-free Scripture discovery produces disciple-making movement formation at rates that appear to exceed alternative models. The proposed research will test this observation rigorously.
