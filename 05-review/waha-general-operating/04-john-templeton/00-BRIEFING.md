## Mission & Priorities (IN THEIR OWN WORDS)

**Mission:** "Prioritizes interdisciplinary and empirical research on religious innovation, or new religious ideas, methods, and practices, that catalyze cultural change."

**Broader stated purpose:** "Fund research and catalyze conversations that inspire people with awe and wonder."

**Core funding areas:**
1. **Religion, Science, and Society** — Research on culture, religious traditions, and spirituality to advance collective understanding of how religious and spiritual beliefs affect human flourishing
2. **Character Virtue Development** — Research and practice supporting character formation, moral reasoning, and virtue in individuals and communities; includes "Cultivating Character in the Digital Age" ($8M initiative)
3. **Life Sciences** — Origins, evolution, and complexity of life
4. **Mathematical and Physical Sciences** — Fundamental questions of physics, cosmology, mathematics
5. **Individual Freedom and Free Markets** — Property rights, rule of law, entrepreneurship
6. **Public Engagement** — Helping the public engage with big questions through media, journalism, and popular culture

**Key recent initiative:** "Cultivating Character in the Digital Age" — $8M in grants studying how AI and digital platforms affect youth character development, virtue, and spiritual formation. Grants went to academic institutions, research nonprofits, and organizations with both research capacity and practical implementation.

## History & Context

Founded by Sir John Templeton (1912–2008), a pioneering mutual fund investor (Franklin Templeton) and committed Christian who believed deeply that science and religion could be complementary, not contradictory. He established the annual Templeton Prize (originally larger than the Nobel Prize in monetary terms) to honor people who make "exceptional contributions to affirming life's spiritual dimension."

The foundation's grantmaking reflects Templeton's conviction that empirical inquiry — done rigorously — can illuminate spiritual questions and that faith and reason need not be enemies. This is not a typical evangelical missionary foundation; it is a research and innovation philanthropy that happens to care deeply about religion.

## Why Waha Is a Fit

Waha represents a genuine opportunity for Templeton — but only if framed correctly. Templeton does not fund ministry delivery alone. They fund projects that advance understanding of how religious innovation works, with empirical evidence.

The framing that unlocks Templeton is: **"Measuring the Impact of Audio-Based Scripture Engagement on Disciple-Making Movements in Oral Cultures."** This reframes Waha from a ministry tool into a research platform. Waha's deployments across 177 countries and 46+ languages represent an unprecedented natural experiment in how audio-based Scripture engagement produces religious innovation, community transformation, and measurable spiritual flourishing. No one has studied this systematically at scale.

Three Templeton program areas connect:

1. **Religion, Science, and Society:** Does audio-based, facilitated Scripture discovery produce different outcomes than text-based, pastor-led Bible teaching? Do oral learners show higher retention, multiplication, and community impact? Waha's 71,000+ Discovery Bible Study meetings across dozens of cultural contexts are an extraordinary dataset waiting for a research partner to analyze.

2. **Character Virtue Development in the Digital Age:** How do mobile apps facilitate spiritual transformation? Does the "gather, press play, discuss" model produce measurable character change — reduced domestic conflict, changed moral behavior, increased prosocial action — among participants? Waha's documented testimonials (addiction ended, marriages restored, former militants transformed) point toward character change data that a research partner could measure systematically.

3. **Religious Innovation:** Waha's model is genuinely innovative: it removes the clergy barrier from Bible study, enables self-directed discovery in any language, and produces multiplication without missionary dependency. This is religious innovation by Templeton's definition — "new religious ideas, methods, and practices that catalyze cultural change."

The key structural requirement: Waha needs an academic partner (seminary, university, missiologist) to co-design the research component. This is not optional for Templeton — it is the gating condition. The research partner provides methodology credibility; Waha provides the deployment infrastructure and data.

## Why They Might Say No

1. **No research component = automatic rejection.** Templeton does not fund ministry delivery alone. The absence of an academic partner with a defined research methodology will result in rejection at the OFI stage. This is the primary risk.

2. **"Empirical research" orientation may conflict with Waha's evangelical framing.** Templeton is comfortable with faith but funds with academic rigor. Proposals that read as missionary advocacy rather than research inquiry will not advance.

3. **Scale mismatch.** Templeton grants are typically $150K–$2M+. Waha's $1.2M total budget and new 501(c)(3) status may raise capacity questions. The proposal must address organizational credibility and the research partner's track record explicitly.

4. **Not primarily an "evangelism" funder.** Templeton funds the study of how religion works in society, not the advancement of Christian evangelism specifically. The proposal must not read as an evangelism fundraising pitch — it must read as a research inquiry into a significant phenomenon.

5. **OFI competition is intense.** 402 grants from a pool that receives thousands of OFIs. Program officers skim OFIs quickly. The first 2-3 sentences must be compelling research questions, not organizational descriptions.
