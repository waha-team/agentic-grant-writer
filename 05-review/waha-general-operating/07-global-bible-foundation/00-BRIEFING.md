## History & Context

Global Bible Foundation appears to be a relatively new, small-scale giving platform operating from Nashville, TN. It functions more like a donor-advised fund (DAF) or giving vehicle than a traditional endowed private foundation — donors contribute, and GBF directs funds to vetted ministry partners. The 94%/6% model is characteristic of giving platforms, not traditional grantmakers.

With only 3 current ministry partners and no public EIN listing in major databases, this is a small, early-stage organization. It is not comparable to large Bible engagement foundations like ETEN Alliance or illumiNations.

**Leadership:** Not publicly identified on their website. No board member names found in public sources.

## Why Waha Is a Fit

Waha's mission aligns directly with Global Bible Foundation's stated purpose:

- **"Bible engagement"** — Waha's Discovery Bible Study app is audio-based Bible engagement for oral learners in 46+ languages
- **"Discipleship worldwide"** — Waha's Disciple Making Course equips believers in 177+ countries
- **"Frontlines of Bible engagement"** — Waha specifically serves restricted-access nations, unreached people groups, and communities without Scripture in their language
- **"Remote villages to bustling cities"** — Waha works offline (SD card, solar player) for communities without internet

Waha's alignment with current partners: All In Ministries International (equipping disciple-makers) and WordSpread (evangelism and discipleship training across Africa) both operate in adjacent mission space to Waha. Waha's Africa presence (Waha case studies from Mauritius, Sub-Saharan Africa, Zimbabwe) and growing African language pipeline (Amharic is the first — more African languages in the 69-language pipeline) strengthens this fit.

**Key differentiator Waha could offer:** An audio-first, offline Bible engagement tool specifically designed for the oral learner populations in Africa that Oasis International and WordSpread already serve. This is a legitimate partnership argument — not just a grant ask.

---

## Why They Might Say No

1. **Small portfolio:** With only 3 current partners, GBF appears to be highly selective. They may not be actively seeking new partners.
2. **Technology vs. print:** Their current partners focus on physical Bibles and in-person training. Waha's audio-app model is a different delivery mechanism — may or may not align with how their donors want their funds used.
3. **No formal grant process:** GBF appears to fund partner organizations by letting donors designate funds to specific partners. Adding a new partner requires vetting and growing their donor base — not just reviewing a grant application.
4. **Size:** GBF may be too small to provide meaningful funding. If their total disbursements are modest (possible given only 3 partners and unclear donor base), the funding potential may not justify Waha's application effort.
5. **No EIN on ProPublica:** Suggests the foundation is either new (hasn't filed 990s yet) or very small (below reporting thresholds). Either limits confidence in their grantmaking capacity.

---

## Recommended Approach

This is a **partnership inquiry**, not a grant application. Waha should:
1. Complete their ministry partnership request form
2. Frame as a mission partnership: "We serve the same people your current partners serve — audio Bible engagement for Africa and beyond"
3. Ask about how GBF's donors could support specific language launches on Waha

**Do not invest Stage 03-05 pipeline resources until partnership contact is made and GBF confirms active grantmaking capacity.** If they respond with interest and meaningful funding potential, advance to full proposal stage. If no response or minimal capacity, archive as a low-priority contact.
