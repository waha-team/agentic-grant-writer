## History & Context

The Huston Foundation was established December 12, 1957, by siblings Charles Lukens Huston, Jr. and Ruth Huston as a tribute to their parents ([Foundation Website — History](https://www.hustonfoundation.org/history.cfm)). The family is rooted in the Lukens Steel Company legacy in Coatesville, Pennsylvania ([Lukens Steel Company Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lukens_Steel_Company)).

**Ruth Huston (1900–1982):** Served over 50 years as a Protestant Evangelical Christian missionary in the Appalachian Mountains of eastern Kentucky. Founded a summer Bible camp, taught Bible studies, allocated most of her income to Christian missions nationally and internationally. Trustee of Bryan College and Bob Jones University. Author of several books including *God's Timing* and *Acting like Christians*. ([Foundation Website — History](https://www.hustonfoundation.org/history.cfm))

**Charles Lukens Huston, Jr. (1906–1982):** Supported educational, community, and faith-based organizations. Founded family service organizations. As president of Lukens Steel, he championed community development and education. ([Foundation Website — History](https://www.hustonfoundation.org/history.cfm))

**Leadership succession:** After the founders' deaths, Nancy G. Huston served as president until her death in 2002. Her three children and five grandchildren now lead the foundation, continuing the founders' mission. ([Foundation Website — History](https://www.hustonfoundation.org/history.cfm))

**Current leadership change:** Charles L. Huston III, who served as Vice President (Community Relations), passed away in January 2025 ([Wilde Funeral Home Obituary](https://www.wildefuneralhome.com/obituaries/charles-l-skip-huston-iii)). The board is now operating with one fewer director.

**Direction note:** The foundation's 2023 grantee list reveals a notable shift toward conservative civic and political organizations alongside traditional evangelical missions work. This may reflect current board priorities under Nancy Huston Hansen's leadership.

## Why They Might Say No

1. **Political orientation shift:** The 2023 grantee list suggests the current board is directing a significant portion of "evangelical" grants toward conservative civic organizations. Waha does not have this profile.
2. **Meeting requirement:** New organizations must meet with Nancy Hansen before applying. If this meeting cannot be arranged before the spring deadline, the earliest realistic cycle is fall 2026.
3. **General operating support preference:** They state "directors prefer to provide funding for specific projects and programs" — general operating support is available but less preferred. Waha should frame the ask around a specific, concrete deliverable (e.g., a new language launch or DMC cohort rollout) even within a general operating support proposal.
4. **No sole-source funding:** They prefer grantees with diverse funding. Waha's 2026 funding plan shows grants as 33% of revenue with multiple sources — this is a positive signal.
5. **Size mismatch risk:** Waha is new (501(c)(3) established 2025) with no 990 filed. First-time grantees typically receive modest amounts ($5,000–$25,000). The ask should be modest for the first application.
6. **Pennsylvania Bureau of Charitable Organizations:** Registration required for applicants (unless exempt). Need to verify whether Waha is registered in PA or qualifies for exemption as an out-of-state organization.
