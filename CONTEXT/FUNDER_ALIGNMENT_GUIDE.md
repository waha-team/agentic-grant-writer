# Waha Funder Alignment Guide

## Purpose

This document equips you to evaluate whether a prospective foundation or funding organization is a strong candidate for Waha grant applications. Our primary funding need is **localizing our Discovery Bible Study (DBS) curriculum and Disciple Making Course (DMC) into new languages**. Use this guide to quickly understand Waha's work, then systematically assess whether a given funder's priorities align with what we do and how we do it.

Read this document in full before beginning your scouting work. Then use the evaluation framework in Part III each time you assess a new foundation.

---

## Part I: Understanding Waha

### What Waha Is

Waha is a free, audio-based mobile app and training platform that empowers ordinary believers to lead Discovery Bible Studies and make disciples — no seminary degree, no ministry training, no special equipment required. The model is simple: **gather, press play, discuss.** A small group opens the app, listens to an audio-guided Bible lesson, pauses to discuss, and applies what they've learned. Anyone who can use a music app like Spotify can facilitate a Waha group.

Waha also offers the **Disciple Making Course (DMC)**, a 9-week interactive training that teaches believers the biblical foundations of disciple-making and sends them out to start their own groups — who in turn start more groups. The vision is multiplication, not addition.

### Who Built It and Who Runs It

Waha is a team of cross-cultural disciple makers and technologists. The app was built by frontline field workers who understood the barriers to disciple-making in hard-to-reach places and designed a tool to break them down. The core team is small (~10 people) and includes roles spanning development, localization, mobilization, regional coordination, and Bible licensing.

Waha is a nonprofit, entirely crowdfunded, and free to all users. There are no ads, no subscriptions, and no paywalls. Donations fund all development and localization work.

### Who Waha Serves

- **Cross-cultural workers** serving in foreign contexts where they may not speak the local language
- **National believers** in unreached and restricted-access regions who want to share their faith but lack resources in their language
- **Oral learners** — people whose primary mode of learning is listening rather than reading, which describes a significant portion of the global population
- **Persecuted believers** in restricted-access nations (RANs) who need discreet, secure tools for discipleship
- **Everyday Christians** — youth pastors, families, laypeople — who want to obey the Great Commission but don't know how to start

### The Problem Waha Solves

There are approximately **7,000 unreached people groups** worldwide — communities with little to no access to the Gospel. Many of these communities are oral cultures. Many are in regions hostile to Christianity. Even where Bible translations exist, getting those translations into the hands of real people in a format they can use remains an enormous gap.

Waha calls this **"the last mile" of Bible translation.** Organizations like Wycliffe, SIL, and the United Bible Societies do the painstaking work of translating Scripture. Waha takes those translations and delivers them to end users in an accessible, audio-based, interactive format that doesn't just put the Bible on a shelf — it puts it into practice through community-based discovery.

### The Localization Model (What We're Funding)

Bringing Waha into a new language is the core of our grant-seeking efforts. Here's what that involves:

- **Curriculum adaptation:** Waha's DBS curriculum and DMC must be translated, culturally adapted, and recorded in audio by native speakers.
- **Bible licensing:** We work with Bible translation organizations to license the specific Scripture passages used in our curriculum. Waha uses approximately **7% of the Bible's verses** — a deliberate strategy that allows us to launch in a language even before a full Bible translation is complete.
- **Audio production:** Every lesson is recorded in-language, because the app is audio-first. This requires native speakers, recording infrastructure, and quality assurance.
- **App localization:** The app interface itself (menus, buttons, navigation) must be translated.
- **Testing and deployment:** Each language launch involves testing with local users, iteration, and rollout.

The time to bring Waha to completion in a new language varies — **from a few months to over a year** depending on the availability of translated Scripture, native speakers, and local partners. The cost-per-language is a concrete, measurable investment with a clear deliverable.

### Key Partnerships

Waha operates within the broader Bible translation and missions ecosystem:

- **Every Tribe Every Nation (ETEN) alliance** — a coalition working to eradicate "Bible poverty" (people without Scripture in their language) by 2033. Waha is part of this effort.
- **Wycliffe, SIL, United Bible Societies** — Waha receives translated Scripture from these organizations and delivers it to end users.
- **Meta** — Waha is a Meta Language Technology Partner, reflecting its commitment to multilingual accessibility.
- **Iran Alive Ministries** — among the ministry partners using Waha for outreach in specific regions.

### Key Design Decisions

These are important for understanding Waha's philosophy and for matching with funders who value these things:

| Design Decision | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Audio-first** | Serves oral learners and low-literacy populations. Doesn't require reading ability. |
| **Offline-capable** | After initial download, everything works without internet. Critical for rural and infrastructure-poor areas. |
| **Security mode** | The app can disguise itself behind a calculator or piano interface, protected by a passcode. Designed for believers in persecuted regions. Vetted by a certified cybersecurity consultant. |
| **SD card and solar player compatible** | Reaches people who lack smartphones, reliable electricity, or internet entirely. |
| **Seeker-friendly** | The app is designed so that a brand-new seeker encountering it for the first time isn't overwhelmed by church jargon or insider language. |
| **No training required to facilitate** | The entire point is that an ordinary person can lead a group. This is what makes multiplication possible. |
| **Free, no ads, no paywalls** | Removes every possible barrier to access. Sustained entirely by donations. |

## Part II: Waha's Identity and Non-Negotiables

Waha is an **explicitly Christian organization** pursuing the Great Commission. This is not a secondary characteristic — it is the entire reason Waha exists. Any funder must be willing to fund work that is, at its core, about making disciples of Jesus Christ.

That said, Waha does not require that funders themselves be faith-based organizations. A secular foundation that funds language preservation, digital literacy, community development, or technology access in underserved regions may be an excellent fit — **as long as they are willing to fund an explicitly Christian organization doing explicitly Christian work.**

The question is not "Is this funder Christian?" The question is: **"Will this funder fund us, knowing exactly what we are?"**

### What Waha Will Not Compromise On

- The app will always be **free**. We will not accept funding that requires paywalling content.
- The content is **biblical**. We will not secularize, generalize, or water down the curriculum to satisfy a funder's preferences.
- The mission is **disciple-making**. We are not a literacy program, a language preservation project, or a technology nonprofit that happens to have religious content. We are a disciple-making organization that uses technology, serves oral learners, and preserves heart languages as part of that mission.

If a funder would need us to reframe our work as something other than what it is, they are not aligned.

## Part III: Funder Evaluation Framework

For each prospective foundation, work through the following evaluation. Score each section, then use the summary rubric at the end to make your recommendation.

### Section A: Mission Alignment

Evaluate how well the funder's stated mission overlaps with Waha's work. Look for alignment with **one or more** of the following themes in the funder's mission statement, grant history, or program areas:

**Primary alignment themes** (directly describe what Waha does):

- [ ] Disciple-making, church planting, or evangelism
- [ ] Bible translation, Scripture access, or Scripture engagement
- [ ] Reaching unreached people groups
- [ ] Supporting work in restricted-access nations or among persecuted believers
- [ ] Multiplying indigenous/national believers and leaders

**Secondary alignment themes** (describe aspects of how Waha works):

- [ ] Multilingual technology or digital tools for underserved language communities
- [ ] Oral cultures, oral learning, or audio-based education
- [ ] Mobile technology for development or community impact
- [ ] Language preservation or mother-tongue resources
- [ ] Digital access and inclusion in the Global South
- [ ] Literacy and education in underserved populations
- [ ] Community development through locally-led initiatives

**Scoring:**
- **Strong** (3 pts): Funder's mission explicitly includes 1+ primary themes
- **Moderate** (2 pts): Funder's mission explicitly includes 2+ secondary themes
- **Weak** (1 pt): Funder's mission touches on 1 secondary theme tangentially
- **None** (0 pts): No discernible overlap

### Section B: Willingness to Fund Faith-Based Work

This is a pass/fail gate. If the answer is "No" or "Unclear and likely No," stop here.

- [ ] Does the funder have a history of funding explicitly religious or faith-based organizations?
- [ ] If not, does the funder's policy explicitly permit grants to religious organizations?
- [ ] If the funder is secular, do their guidelines prohibit funding proselytization, evangelism, or religious instruction? *(If yes, Waha is likely disqualified — our curriculum is inherently evangelistic and discipleship-oriented.)*

**Scoring:**
- **Pass**: Funder clearly funds or is open to funding faith-based work, including evangelism/discipleship
- **Conditional Pass**: Funder is secular but does not prohibit faith-based grantees; further research needed on their specific restrictions
- **Fail**: Funder explicitly excludes religious activities, proselytization, or faith-based programming

### Section C: Geographic and Demographic Fit

Evaluate whether the funder's geographic and demographic priorities match where Waha operates and who it serves.

- [ ] Does the funder prioritize the **Global South**, developing nations, or specific regions where Waha has strong presence or expansion plans?
- [ ] Does the funder focus on **marginalized, underserved, or hard-to-reach populations**?
- [ ] Does the funder prioritize **indigenous communities or minority language groups**?
- [ ] Does the funder have interest in **oral cultures or low-literacy populations**?

**Scoring:**
- **Strong** (3 pts): Funder's geographic/demographic focus maps closely to Waha's operational regions and target populations
- **Moderate** (2 pts): Partial overlap in geography or demographics
- **Weak** (1 pt): Funder operates globally with no specific focus, or focuses on regions where Waha has limited presence
- **None** (0 pts): Funder's focus is exclusively on regions or populations Waha does not serve

### Section D: Grant Type and Size Fit

Evaluate whether the funder's typical grant structure works for Waha's localization projects.

- [ ] Does the funder offer **project-based grants** (as opposed to only general operating support or capital campaigns)?
- [ ] Is the funder's typical grant size in a range that could meaningfully fund one or more language localizations?
- [ ] Does the funder support **technology development, content creation, or media production** projects?
- [ ] Is the grant timeline compatible with localization work (which can span months to over a year)?

**Scoring:**
- **Strong** (3 pts): Grant type, size, and timeline are a natural fit for a localization project
- **Moderate** (2 pts): Workable with some framing or scope adjustment
- **Weak** (1 pt): Possible but would require significant proposal creativity
- **None** (0 pts): Grant structure fundamentally incompatible

### Section E: Strategic Value Beyond Funding

Consider whether a relationship with this funder offers additional value.

- [ ] Does the funder have a **network or portfolio** that could connect Waha with translation partners, field workers, or regional contacts?
- [ ] Does the funder have **credibility or brand recognition** that would strengthen Waha's reputation with other funders?
- [ ] Does the funder offer **capacity-building support** (e.g., organizational development, reporting frameworks, strategic planning) in addition to money?
- [ ] Could this funder become a **long-term partner** for multiple language launches, rather than a one-time grant?

**Scoring:**
- **High** (3 pts): Significant strategic value beyond the grant itself
- **Some** (2 pts): Moderate additional value
- **Minimal** (1 pt): Standard grant relationship, no special strategic benefit
- **None** (0 pts): No additional value

### Decision Matrix

| Section B Result | Total Score (A+C+D+E) | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **Fail** | Any | **Discard.** Do not pursue. |
| **Pass** | 9-12 | **Strong candidate.** Prioritize this application. |
| **Pass** | 6-8 | **Moderate candidate.** Worth pursuing if bandwidth allows. |
| **Pass** | 3-5 | **Weak candidate.** Pursue only if no stronger options exist. |
| **Pass** | 0-2 | **Discard.** Insufficient alignment. |
| **Conditional Pass** | 9-12 | **Investigate further.** High potential but verify faith-based policy before investing time in an application. |
| **Conditional Pass** | 6-8 | **Low priority.** Only investigate if strong candidates are exhausted. |
| **Conditional Pass** | 0-5 | **Discard.** Not worth the risk of a policy mismatch. |

## Part V: Quick-Reference — How to Describe Waha to a Funder

When reading a foundation's priorities and mentally matching them against Waha, it helps to have multiple "lenses" for describing our work. Waha sits at the intersection of several domains, and different funders will resonate with different framings. **All of the following descriptions are true and accurate** — choose the lens that best matches what a given funder cares about:

| If the funder cares about... | Waha is... |
|---|---|
| The Great Commission / evangelism | A disciple-multiplication platform that empowers any believer to make disciples who make disciples, available in 46+ languages |
| Bible translation / Scripture access | The "last mile" delivery system that puts translated Scripture into practice through community-based discovery, partnered with the ETEN alliance |
| Unreached people groups | A tool designed to reach the 7,000 unreached people groups by equipping national believers with audio-based discipleship curriculum in their heart language |
| Persecuted church / restricted nations | A security-hardened discipleship app with encryption, disguise mode, and offline capability — purpose-built for believers in hostile environments |
| Technology for development | A free, offline-capable, audio-first mobile platform serving low-literacy and low-connectivity populations in 177+ countries |
| Language preservation / multilingual access | A multilingual content platform delivering mother-tongue audio curriculum, with a localization pipeline expanding from 46 languages toward hundreds |
| Oral cultures / oral education | An audio-based learning platform designed specifically for oral learners who absorb information through listening and discussion rather than reading |
| Community-led development | A locally-owned model where national believers lead their own groups with no dependency on foreign workers or institutions |

**Important:** Never misrepresent Waha. Every framing above is genuine. But if a funder's requirements would need you to hide or downplay the evangelistic and discipleship nature of the work, that funder is not aligned. Move on.
